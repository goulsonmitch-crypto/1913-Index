The Engine

The Federal Reserve

On December 23, 1913, Congress created the Federal Reserve and handed a small group the power to expand the money supply at will. The promise was stability — an end to panics, a guardian of the dollar. The record since is a dollar that has lost the overwhelming majority of its purchasing power.

  • 1913 Federal Reserve Act signed. The dollar is still defined as gold.
  • 1933 Domestic gold ownership banned; the dollar devalued from $20.67 to $35 per ounce.
  • 1971 President Nixon "temporarily" ends gold convertibility. The dollar becomes pure fiat. Inflation accelerates.
  • 2008 The Fed's balance sheet explodes through "quantitative easing" — trillions in new money.
  • 2020–22 Roughly 40% of all dollars in existence are created in two years. The worst inflation in four decades follows.

The strongest case for the Fed: defenders argue a central bank smooths panics, acts as lender of last resort, and that moderate, predictable inflation (~2%) greases a modern economy and avoids ruinous deflationary spirals. The pre-Fed era had its own brutal banking panics.

The rebuttal: "moderate" inflation is still a continuous, compounding tax on every saver — a transfer from people who hold dollars to whoever spends the new ones first. A 2% target is not price stability; it is a policy of guaranteed, perpetual erosion. The panics the Fed was sold to prevent kept happening — 1929, 2008, 2020 — only now they're met with more money creation, which shows up later as higher prices on the shelf. The 1913 Index exists to measure that bill.
