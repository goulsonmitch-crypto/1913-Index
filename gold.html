The dollar, measured in gold

Before 1913 a dollar was a fixed weight of gold — $20.67 bought a full ounce. The Federal Reserve, and the slow severing of the dollar from gold (finished in 1971), changed that. Priced in the one money the Fed cannot print, here is what the dollar has done.

The dollar has lost — of its value against gold since the Fed was founded.

An ounce of gold cost in 1913. Today that same ounce costs — the metal didn't change; the dollar did. Gold is not "going up." The yardstick is shrinking.

  • 1913 Dollar defined as gold; $20.67 = one ounce.
  • 1933 Americans barred from owning monetary gold; dollar devalued to $35/oz.
  • 1971 Nixon ends gold convertibility. The dollar becomes pure fiat.

The mainstream view: gold is a volatile commodity, not a practical everyday measure, and a gold standard can import deflation and tie a central bank's hands in a crisis. Most economists prefer a flexible fiat currency.

The sound-money reply: "flexibility" is the whole problem — it is the freedom to create money, which is the freedom to dilute yours. Gold's "volatility" against the dollar is largely the dollar moving, not the gold. A standard that ties the printer's hands is a feature, not a bug, to anyone who saves in dollars.
