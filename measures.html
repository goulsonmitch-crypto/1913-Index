What each measure is — and which way it leans

The 1913 Index ↑ highest

A fixed basket at CPI's own weights, measured in the real prices of the same goods, with real home prices replacing the OER survey. Answers: how much more do the actual same things cost in actual dollars.

Official CPI-U ↓ lower

The government's headline. Measures the cost of an equivalent standard of living, applying substitution, hedonic quality adjustments, and OER for housing — all of which pull the number down relative to raw prices.

Core CPI pending data feed

CPI excluding food and energy (the volatile parts). Used by the Fed to read the trend. Usually close to headline CPI over long spans; smoother year to year.

Chained CPI (C-CPI-U) ↓ lowest official

Like CPI but lets the basket re-weight as consumers substitute toward cheaper goods every period. Structurally the lowest official measure — now used to index tax brackets, which is why it matters who picks the formula.

PCE price index pending data feed

The Fed's preferred 2% target gauge. Broader scope (includes employer-paid healthcare), different weights, and more substitution than CPI — typically runs a few tenths below CPI.

"1980-method" reconstruction ↑ highest claimed

Re-computes inflation using the pre-1983 methodology (housing by home prices not OER, fewer hedonic/substitution adjustments) — the idea behind ShadowStats, but we'll do it openly and reproducibly from sourced components rather than as a black box. Expected to run well above headline CPI. Built once the component feed is connected.

The typical good computed below

A bottom-up check: the median annual price rise across every individual good and service we track (excluding currencies/crypto). Not weighted like CPI — just "what did the middle item actually do." A reality check on the basket measures.
