Methodology & Honesty

Credibility is the whole point. Three rules govern everything here:

1. Real prices, not hypotheticals. We use actual transaction prices wherever they exist. Most importantly, we replace CPI's Owners' Equivalent Rent — a survey of imagined rents — with the real sale price of a home.

2. Every figure is sourced, rated, and reproducible. Each item shows its source and a confidence rating. Estimates and pre-1950 figures are flagged. The raw data lives in a single file anyone can audit.

3. We steelman the other side first. Before criticizing an official number, we state the strongest version of its defense. If a government figure is actually fair, we say so — that honesty is what makes the rest land.

How the number is computed

The freshness model

Official figures (BLS, Census, USPS) drive the headline index, so it is maximally citable. The live counters — gold and gasoline — use the freshest daily/weekly number. Scheduled jobs refresh the prices weekly and the category indexes quarterly.

Verification & audit — how every figure earns its confidence rating

CPI is a black box: you cannot see how any single number was built. We do the opposite. Every one of the items in the dataset runs through an automated audit that anyone can re-run from the open script (scripts/verify.js), and each carries a confidence tier shown on the data page and on its own item page.

Two independent checks run on every item:

1. Internal audit (no outside data needed). We flag any item whose implied inflation diverges sharply from comparable goods in its own category — a statistical outlier test that catches data-entry errors and surfaces genuinely unusual items. Plus basic sanity checks (no non-positive prices, no impossible rates) and a check for whether the item has a real per-year price series behind it.

2. External cross-source. For as many items as we can, we confirm today's price against a fully independent second source — gasoline checked against EIA where the primary is AAA; gold against an independent spot feed; food against USDA. When two unrelated sources agree within 5%, the figure is marked cross-verified. When a second source disagrees by more than 10%, it is flagged conflict and held for correction — we would rather show a disagreement than hide it.

The five confidence tiers (best to weakest):

● Cross-verified — an independent second source confirms the current price within 5%.

● Audited (item-level) — passes every internal check and has a real, item-specific BLS price series (the green line on its chart).

● Category model — endpoints are sourced, but the year-by-year path follows the item's broad BLS category index (a labeled proxy, drawn as a blue dashed line), not an item-specific series. Honest and better than a pure model — but not the same as item-level data.

● Plausible — passes the internal audit; no second source or series yet.

● Review — flagged as a category outlier or by a sanity check; awaiting a human or second-source look.

● Conflict — a second source actively disagrees by more than 10%.

Scheduled jobs grow this automatically: a monthly harvester backfills real per-year series, and a weekly cross-source job adds independent second prices and re-runs the audit. The number of cross-verified figures only goes up over time.

Source-quality tiers

Tier 1 · Gold Standard — an authoritative, continuous official series (e.g., BLS Average Price Data, Census, EIA). Tier 2 · Sourced — real data with gaps, a definition shift, or a documented splice (e.g., the Shiller-derived 1913 home price). Tier 3 · Reconstructed — built from catalogs, menus, ads, or estimates. Estimates and pre-1950 figures are flagged.

Common criticisms — answered

"Why not just use CPI?" We do use CPI's structure — the same eight major groups at CPI's own weights. We change only what CPI measures with a hypothetical: housing. CPI fills ~a third of its index with Owners' Equivalent Rent, a survey asking homeowners to imagine their home's rent. We use the real sale price instead.

"Why these categories and weights?" They are not ours — they are BLS's official relative-importance weights (Dec 2024). Using CPI's own basket is what makes this a fair, apples-to-apples replacement rather than a cherry-pick.

"What about quality improvements (hedonics)?" For the new BLS categories we keep CPI's quality adjustments as-is — so our number is, if anything, conservative there. We only strip hedonics where it most distorts lived cost: cars, where we use raw transaction prices, because you cannot actually buy the 1913 level of car anymore.

"Isn't gold cherry-picked?" Gold is shown on its own page as a sound-money lens, not as a basket item — it does not touch the headline index. The index is built from consumer prices, not gold.

"Aren't wages up too?" Yes — and we show exactly that on the affordability page, in hours of work. Many goods are cheaper in labor terms; housing, healthcare, and college are not. We don't hide the parts that cut against us.

This is a living prototype. The basket now mirrors CPI's full structure; figures, sourcing, and the historical series keep improving. We ship the good rather than wait on the perfect.
