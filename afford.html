The Honest Test

Priced in hours of work

Dollars lie; time doesn't. The fairest test of affordability isn't the price tag — it's how many hours of work it takes to earn it.

While some would say everything has gotten cheaper — set the baseline to 1913 below and food, fuel, and manufactured goods all take fewer work-hours than they did a century ago, because real wages rose enormously — that picture flips the moment you move the baseline to 1971, the year the dollar went fully fiat. Cheap consumer goods keep falling, but housing, cars, and fuel cost more of your working life than they did when the printing press lost its last restraint. The free market makes things cheaper; the Fed-and-credit-driven essentials go the other way.

Hours = price ÷ the average hourly wage of a typical worker that year (BLS; pre-1964 estimated). Green = cheaper in work-hours today; red = costs more of your life. Non-anchor baseline prices are estimated from each item's real category price history.
