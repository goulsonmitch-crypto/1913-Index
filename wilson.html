The Origin

1913: the year the modern fiscal state was built

This index is named for 1913 for a reason. In a single stretch under one administration — Woodrow Wilson's — the country acquired three permanent fixtures it has never escaped, and together they explain how the government funds itself to this day.

  • 1913 The federal income tax (16th Amendment) — a permanent, direct claim on the fruits of your labor.
  • 1913 The Federal Reserve — the money-printing engine this entire site measures.
  • 1917 Entry into World War I — mass conscription, the Sedition Act, and the template for the wartime administrative state.

From this trio flow the two great mechanisms by which government funds itself without honest consent: tax what you earn, and inflate what you save. The income tax takes openly; the Fed takes quietly. Together they are why a 1913 dollar is worth pennies today.

The through-line. While some would say Wilson earns his high ranking — for the League of Nations vision, the Progressive-era reforms, and steering the country through a world war, with the income tax and the Fed enjoying bipartisan support as modernizations of federal finance — "modernized federal finance" is a gentle way to describe building the machinery of permanent taxation and currency debasement, in the same year, 1913, that gives this index its name. You can admire parts of the era and still see the pattern plainly: from that year forward, the state could tax what you earn and inflate what you save. Everything this site measures is the bill, still arriving in your grocery cart.
