One national figure hides everything

CPI gives the whole country one housing number. Reality is not one number.

CPI's shelter component is a single national average. But the cost of a home didn't move the same everywhere: since 2000, real home prices (Case-Shiller, repeat-sales) roughly doubled in Chicago and more than quadrupled in Miami. Why this matters

This is a small starter set of five metros, not the whole country — we'll say so plainly. But it already makes the point a single national CPI figure cannot: the housing inflation a family actually felt depended enormously on where they lived. The same years that nearly doubled Chicago more than quadrupled Miami. The 1913 Index treats that spread as signal, not noise — because it's the difference between a number being technically accurate and being true to anyone's life.

Roadmap: extend to all 20 Case-Shiller metros, add metro-level CPI, and let any user pull their city's full 1913-style basket.
