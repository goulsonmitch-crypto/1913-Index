A third of CPI isn't a price

The biggest piece of CPI is a survey of imagined rents

For housing — the largest component of the index — CPI doesn't use what homes cost. It uses Owners' Equivalent Rent (OER): a survey asking homeowners to estimate what their home would rent for. That estimate fills about a third of the entire index. Here is the real thing instead: the actual median home price and the actual monthly mortgage payment, straight from FRED.

What this does and doesn't show

While some would say the gap here is small — over long spans OER and real home prices aren't wildly apart, with the median home up about — since 1983 versus OER's — — OER is still a survey of imagined rents, not a price, and it fills the single biggest slot in CPI; substituting a real, observable price for an imagined one is simply more honest. It also smooths away the booms families actually live through: engineered to be slow and rent-like, it muted the 2000s bubble and the 2020–22 surge, exactly the episodes that priced people out. And the real cost of buying isn't the price alone — it's the monthly payment, which swings violently with mortgage rates. The 1913 Index uses real home prices, not OER: the gap from this one swap is real but moderate, and the honesty of the swap is what makes the rest of the index credible.
