The locked methodology (why this is the number)

The headline is a fixed basket measured at CPI's own official relative-importance weights — the same eight major groups the Bureau of Labor Statistics uses, in the same proportions. We change exactly one thing, and we change it in the open:

The one substitution — housing. CPI fills about a third of its entire index with Owners' Equivalent Rent: a survey asking homeowners to imagine what their home would rent for. We replace that imagined figure with the real sale price of a home (Case–Shiller / Census). That single, transparent swap is the core of the gap.

We considered three ways to build the headline — a fixed basket, a user-adjustable basket, and a pure item-by-item average. We locked the fixed CPI-weighted basket as the official number, because using CPI's own structure is what makes this a fair, apples-to-apples replacement rather than a cherry-pick. The adjustable basket and the raw item list remain available as exploration tools — but the citable figure is the fixed one, so it can't be accused of being tuned to a conclusion.

The basket, in full

Each category is measured by its real BLS price index since 1913; shelter uses real home prices (not OER); transportation uses raw car & gas prices. Weights are BLS's published relative importances.

The secondary anchor — 1971

The Fed's founding (1913) is the headline anchor. The second anchor is 1971, when the dollar's last tie to gold was cut. Since 1971 the 1913 Index shows about — against CPI's — — the sound-money story in one comparison.

Where we agree with the government

Credibility is the whole point, so we say plainly where CPI is fair: food has risen roughly in line with the official number, and several goods — electronics, clothing, some appliances — are genuinely cheaper, in dollars and in hours of work. The gap is concentrated where it hurts most: housing, healthcare, and college. We don't hide the parts that cut against us; see the hours-of-work lens and the full dataset.
