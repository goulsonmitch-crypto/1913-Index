"Wages went up too" — so let's measure it honestly

A raise that buys less is not a raise

The standard reply to any inflation complaint is "but wages rose too." True — the average hourly wage for ordinary (production & non-supervisory) workers went from $2.54 in 1964 to $31.34 in 2025, about 12×. The honest question isn't whether the number got bigger; it's what an hour of work actually buys. Hours of work is the one unit the Fed can't print, so we price the big things in hours.

The honest reading

While some would say wages more than kept up — measured against some goods (a TV, a long-distance call, a basic calculator) an hour of work buys far more than it did in 1964, because technology genuinely got cheaper and CPI is right to capture that — the things that anchor a stable life now cost far more hours than they used to. The same median house that took about — hours of work in 1964 takes roughly — today: nearly — the working time. That is the gap a single "real wages are up slightly" headline buries — and it's why the 1913 Index prices the essentials in hours, not just dollars.
